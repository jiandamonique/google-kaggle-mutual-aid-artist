// about this project

Built from lived experience
and open-source care

The Artist Mutual Aid Directory was born during the 2021 Kaggle/Google BIPOC Data Fellowship. The idea was simple: use data science to make it easier for BIPOC and LGBTQ+ artists to find the help that already exists.

The fellowship ended. The need didn't. Five years later, the project is being revived — same architecture, deeper roots.

"Each one, reach one. Each one, teach one."

🌱 The Vision

A zero-dependency, open-source resource directory that any artist can use, any community can fork, and any mutual aid network can contribute to.

🔬 The Method

Python · BeautifulSoup · Pandas → structured data → vanilla HTML/CSS/JS. No framework. No server. Zippable, forkable, lightweight by design.

👤 Who It's For

BIPOC artists. LGBTQ+ creatives. Anyone in the arts who needs housing, healthcare, legal help, or emergency funds — and doesn't know where to start.

🤝 Built By

Jianda Monique — Jr. Data Scientist, author, musician, and multidisciplinary artist. 2021 Kaggle/Google BIPOC Data Fellow. Mentored by Prasun Kumar Mishra and Julia Elliott.

Data Sources

The 2021 plan assumed bulk public datasets would supply most of this directory's content. That landscape has changed since — bulk dataset search has gotten significantly harder, and some previously reliable sources turned out to be inconsistent or unmaintained. So this directory now runs on manually vetted data: every resource listed has been individually researched, not bulk-imported.

A note on asking for help

You may have been told — directly or indirectly — that needing help is a character flaw. It isn't. Mutual aid exists precisely because systems fail people. Asking is not weakness. Asking is how the network works.

You may also encounter compassion fatigue — in your community, in your friends, even in yourself. People who love you may still be unable to give. That is not a verdict on your worth or your need. Keep looking. The resource exists. This primer will help you find it.

// section 1

How to Find Resources

Navigating mutual aid and emergency resource directories can feel overwhelming — especially when you are already in crisis. Here is a simple framework.

1

Start with 211

211.org is the fastest on-ramp. It connects you to local services — food, housing, utilities, mental health — by zip code. Call, text, or use the website.

2

Search by category, not just keyword

Most directories organize by need type: housing, healthcare, grants, direct financial aid, legal. Try searching the category you need most rather than a broad term like "help."

3

Check eligibility before you apply

Many resources are specific — BIPOC artists, LGBTQ+ individuals, residents of a particular city. Read the eligibility criteria first to save yourself time and energy.

4

Ask in community spaces

Facebook groups, Discord servers, mutual aid networks, and local artist collectives often know about resources that never make it to official directories. Ask openly. Someone knows.

5

Document everything

Keep a simple log of what you applied for, when, and any reference numbers. Follow up. Systems are slow and understaffed — persistence matters.

What Mutual Aid Is

A living idea, not a fixed definition.

Mutual aid resists a single clean definition — and that resistance is intentional. Need is individual. Community is local. What works in one neighborhood may not work in another. What follows are guidelines, not rules.

It is reciprocal, not charitable

Charity flows one direction — from those who have, to those who don't. Mutual aid moves in all directions. The person receiving today may be the person giving tomorrow. There is no hierarchy of worthiness.

It is community-driven, not institution-driven

Mutual aid networks are built by the people who use them. They respond faster, more personally, and more flexibly than institutions — because they are accountable to neighbors, not to boards or grant cycles.

It is political by nature

Mutual aid acknowledges that people are in need because systems have failed them — not because of personal failing. It does not ask people to be deserving. It asks only that we show up for each other.

It includes you

If you are building a mutual aid network, organizing resources, or doing the labor of care — you are also allowed to receive. Paying yourself for your labor is ethical. Accepting support while you give it is not a contradiction. It is the whole point.

On compassion fatigue — and the people who make it harder

Sometimes the people closest to you run out of capacity to give. Sometimes they express that unkindly. Friends can be petty about fundraising. People who love you can still say the wrong thing when you ask for help. This is real, it is painful, and it is worth naming out loud.

Compassion fatigue is a genuine phenomenon — not an excuse, but a real limitation. It does not mean your need is too much. It means the people around you are also carrying weight. Widen the net. Look beyond your immediate circle. The resource exists.
